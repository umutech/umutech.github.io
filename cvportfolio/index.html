Sensor based robotic vehicle: (First Year group project)

Tasked with developing a vehicle that can detect and decode signals (such as Radio (RF), Ultrasound, Infrared and Magnetic Fields). The decoded data then needs to be wirelessly transmitted to the user after post-processing on an onboard micro controller.

Sensor Arm: Contains an antenna, hall sensor, photo diodes and other sensors. The sensor arm can be rotated by an onboard motor to improve signal reception.

Post-processing board: Onboard Micro controller post-processes data after being demodulated and decoded by the circuitry.

Custom Chassis: The case was custom designed on CAD software then 3D printed.

Modular Design: Incorporated a modular design where each circuit board can be changed out when needed. The battery is also user removable to enhance user self-maintenance.

Hand-soldered: Each circuit was hand soldered after being tested on simulation software and a first prototype. Finally, each board connects to a central ‘main’ board to which the micro controller is connected to.

High Voltage Power Supply (Flyback Converter)

Group collaboration to build a high voltage power supply in order to work on other electricity concepts and carry out experiments.

Voltage Booster: Takes 12 – 24V DC Input then boosts this voltage to 20,000V DC.

Feedback mechanism: Feedback coil takes the input and makes it an high oscillating signal. This is required to drive the push-pull transistor configuration.

‘Fly-back’ Transformer: Output of the transistors is connected to the primary winding of the transformer. The transformer boosts the voltage to 20,000V. This voltage is turned into DC via an internal diode.

This high voltage power supply has led to other exciting group projects such as building a Tesla coil and Ionic wind generator

Other projects include repairing and restoring a faulty gaming system into working order and understanding how its cooling system works. One reason why these models become faulty is because of CPU overheating.  


Rectifying the overheating issue: After some research, it was understood that bad thermal contact with the heat sink caused this issue. This project aimed to upgrade the thermal performance. One way to remove the processor ‘heat spreader’ lid, a technique that is high in risk if done incorrectly.

Modifications: A modification can also be done to increase the airflow into the cooling system. These alterations greatly reduced the internal temperature of the system, leading to a longer life span of the device.

Analogue/RF (RFIC) Design Project: (Summer 2020 Internship)

Tasked with developing a low-power Operational Trans-conductance Amplifier in TSMC 40nm ULP. Challenge included 900mV supply rail, low noise and high stability (130MHz bandwidth and 80 degrees phase-margin)

Potential applications included a buffer stage for an CMOS ADC. Circuit had to be robust and work as expected across all corners. Final design would be implemented in Layout and results presented to Principal Engineers.

Design: Op-Amp was designed and simulated in Virtuoso ADE with a mixture of devices used (AVT and LVT transistors in the 40nm Library). Bias Voltage Generator and Current mirror circuits were implemented.

The circuit was configured in a unity gain follower configuration to simulate behaviour if used as a buffer.

Statistical Analysis: Monte-Carlo simulations were done, and necessary corrections made to ensure design was robust. Final pre-layout circuit had all important parameters within 98% of their 3-sigma range. Majority of process corners had on average 98%- 100% yield output.

Layout: Common-Centroid and Mismatch techniques applied in order to optimise layout performance and decrease in the impact of process variations. Above example is from main NMOS current and bias generator circuit block.

Floor-Plan: A floorplan was created and routing between common circuit blocks took place. Virtuoso Layout tool was used to produce the layout and tools such as “Module Generator” were used to implement some of the mismatch techniques such as Common-Centroid.


Final Routing was then subjected to LVS and DRC tests. A final PEX (XRC) simulation was carried out.

Final Bode Plot: The final bode plot is shown on the left. Final PEX Simulation at all corners resulted in average gain of 80dB at typical corners and minimum of 130 MHz and 80 degrees phase margin whilst maintaining low noise. Final optimisations were made, and results presented to Principal Engineers.

Electro-Mechanical Math Aid for Blind People: (Second Year Group Project)

During our second-year group project, we designed a portable electro-mechanical device for Blind users to sense mathematical functions through a dynamic tactile interface. Mostly responsible for the electro-mechanical mechanism and Control and Power circuitry as well as co-designing the case and tactile elements. Several prototypes were made and documented. The final prototype can be seen below.

Enclosure: Final device was enclosed in a solid plastic casing. The tactile elements were dome shaped to help ergonomics and was 3D printed.


Any external buttons had a physical Braille representation engraved onto the device to ease user accessibility.

Battery: Device can be used in a portable environment. A battery system was designed and can easily be user replaced if needed.

Prototypes: Several prototypes were made to validate new methods to move the tactile elements. An initial “development kit” was made for the software team to work on creating an application. The final design used 360- degrees stepper motors to enhance accuracy due to controllable nature of the motors.

Internal Mechanism: Stepper Belts and Gears were used. All parts were designed, and 3D printed.

Electronics: Control and power circuitry implemented to accurately control the motors. A built-in calibration system was designed to calibrate the motors and store these values in memory. Location of Motors and tactile elements was realised using current sensing of the motors. This signal was then connected to a micro controller to detect the position of each motor to calibrate it.

Gain Stages: Low noise gain stages used at first stage with supply bypass.

FPGA Synthesizer/Signal Generator & USRP QAM Receiver: (Lab Projects)

Several experiments and projects using FPGA based devices including a USRP Software Defined Radio (SDR) kit and Altera Cyclone V FPGA development board. Projects included building a signal generator and voice synthesizer used in conjunction with an ADC & DAC on the Cyclone V FPGA. The USRP kit was used with the NI LabView suite in order to create an FM/AM transceiver and a multi-bit QAM transceiver. These implementations also included error correction.

Building Sine Generator: A sine-wave generator was designed and implemented on the FPGA. This was mainly done but implementing a look-up table and storing this in the ROM. The required frequency can be achieved by “stretching” the signal outputted, effectively increasing the time period.

Low Pass: A final low pass removes any high frequency components caused by the DAC process.

Software Defined Radio: Modern communication methods were implemented onto the USRP (Programmable Radio) device including techniques to reduce error.

QAM / AM / FM Implementations: A QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation) transceiver was implemented and shown to academics to explain the difficulty of interference and noise immunity as higher rates of QAM are used (16QAM, 64 QAM and so on.)
