Sensor based robotic vehicle: (First Year group project)

Tasked with developing a vehicle that can detect and decode signals (such as Radio (RF), Ultrasound, Infrared and Magnetic Fields). The decoded data then needs to be wirelessly transmitted to the user after post-processing on an onboard micro controller.

Post-processing board: Onboard Micro controller post-processes data after being demodulated and decoded by the circuitry.

Custom Chassis: The case was custom designed on CAD software then 3D printed.

Modular Design: Incorporated a modular design where each circuit board can be changed out when needed. The battery is also user removable to enhance user self-maintenance.

Hand-soldered: Each circuit was hand soldered after being tested on simulation software and a first prototype. Finally, each board connects to a central ‘main’ board to which the micro controller is connected to.

High Voltage Power Supply (Flyback Converter)

Group collaboration to build a high voltage power supply in order to work on other electricity concepts and carry out experiments.

Voltage Booster: Takes 12 – 24V DC Input then boosts this voltage to 20,000V DC.

Feedback mechanism: Feedback coil takes the input and makes it an high oscillating signal. This is required to drive the push-pull transistor configuration.

‘Fly-back’ Transformer: Output of the transistors is connected to the primary winding of the transformer. The transformer boosts the voltage to 20,000V. This voltage is turned into DC via an internal diode.

This high voltage power supply has led to other exciting group projects such as building a Tesla coil and Ionic wind generator

FinFET and GAAFET TCAD Project: (Third Year Semiconductor Electronics Project)

Explored the limitations of traditional CMOS technologies such as standard Planar MOSFET technology and finFET technology when downscaling below 200nm. A Group Project was carried out to explore these limitations and investigate how these can be overcome. Most of the limitations were caused by the Drain Induced Barrier Lowering (DIBL) Effect.

Jump to Top

The simulations were carried out using the Synopsys Sentaurus TCAD Suite. Alongside gaining experience using the TCAD Suite, experience was gained scripting and developing various semiconductor models for use with TCAD as well as the different Quantum and Electrical parameters used in the simulations.

Downscaling down to 175nm: A base planar 175nm MOSFET was taken and improved using various techniques such as halo doping and side-wall spacer technology. The improved result can be seen here with the base and improved 175nm MOSFET.


Exploring Self Heating Effects of GAAFETS: Since the nanowire GAAFET is surrounded by an oxide, self-heating is an important issue. The project investigated the Peltier and Joule heating in the Nanowire. Useful experience was gained in programming the TCAD script to produce the GAAFET with various physics parameters implemented.

Once it was clear that standard planar CMOS technology would not be sufficient for deep sub-micron technologies, finFET and GAAFET technology was investigated. A Group Project was then carried out to investigate the GAAFET, particularly nano-wire GAAFETs. The electrical characteristics were investigated. At the end of the project, our group produced a 10-page journal detailing our investigations into the self-heating effects of the nanowire GAAFET. This is an upcoming technology with real potential to be used in 3nm and below process nodes.

Analogue/RF Vector Network Analyser Project (Third Year Instrumentation Project)

During the Third Year Instrumentation module, an individual project was assigned. The project was to design and simulate a inexpensive Vector Network Analyser that could measure the Y Parameters of various 2 port DUTs. The device was required to measure from DC to 200 kHz with a challenge to make the device work up-to 100 MHz. Both frequency ranges were explored in this project and simulated on the SPICE simulation engine.

Jump to Top

Low  Frequency Schematic (High Level) for frequencies DC - 500 kHz

High Frequency Schematic (High Level) for frequencies 500 kHz - 100 MHz kHz

Low Frequency Implementation: The Low Frequency implementation was accomplished using the traditional Auto Balancing Bridge method. A pair of trans-impedance amplifiers were used to measure the current flowing through the DUT on both ends (assuming a common ground on the remaining two pins). The measured currents are then split into their Magnitude and Phase components using custom made Phase and Magnitude detection blocks. Instrumentation amplifiers were used to ensure minimal loading on the DUT as well as offer accurate current readings.

High Frequency Implementation: A similar approach was used however using a passive arrangement of measuring the current (as an active solution will not suffice for the high frequency range). The Phase and Magnitude detector blocks, automatically switch to their High Frequency mode. Since the instrumentation amplifier can no longer be used in this case, a series of Low Noise Amplifiers (LNA) are used to amplify the current and voltage signals. The current is measured using a sense resistor.

Micro-Controller Calibration Techniques and Implementation

Implementation of Calibration: A High-Rate ADC was proposed to be used in between the RF Front End and Microcontroller Unit (similarly seen in Low End Rigol Oscilloscopes). For simulation purposes, MATLAB was used as a microcontroller substitute. Phase and Magnitude Voltage Readings are imported into MATLAB. Calibration works by analysing the expected Phase and Magnitude against what is measured. A relationship is then extracted using a n-th order regression (Interpolation and Extrapolation). Typically, first order was sufficient however for higher frequencies, third order was used for higher accuracy.

Once the Calibration algorithm was implemented. The whole system was put together. A flow chart of the operation of the proposed system is shown here. Note that the device operates within DC to 100 MHz and was tested for a variety of 2-port DUT devices such as Resistive and Capacitive Tee Networks.

The final device offered fast and accurate results for the various Y-Parameters. The percentage error can be as low as 0.05%. The two methods implemented for Low Frequency and High Frequency, respectively, offer the highest precision for their frequency ranges. Here we can see the Percentage Error at 100 kHz.

High Performance Switch Mode ATX Power Supply (Third Year Power Electronics Project)

A Design Project was carried out for the Third Year Power Electronics module. The project consisted of developing a low-cost ATX Power Supply based on a custom Switch Mode Power Supply architecture. The design challenge included a £150 Component limit, as well as implementing an Active PFC solution to ensure the current drain is mostly sinusoidal and complies with the EN61000 standard. The final device must have an efficiency of at least 85% at full load.

Jump to Top

The design utilised a Boost PFC stage as the input stage to ensure the device complies with the EN61000 regulation as well as to ensure the ATX Power Supply can operate from AC voltages of 100V to 300V AC. A stacked flyback transformer configuration was used in conjunction with a push-pull primary side.

The device outputs 5 different rails, capable of outputting over 650W in total. The system is completely stable within 120ms of start-up and offers minimal ripple on the output rails. The control system implemented was fully custom designed and was based on a state-space solution. The controller and MOSFET gate driver operate at 130kHz. Through-out the project, a great insight was given into the suitability of various Power Semiconductor devices such as IGBT’s and Power MOSFETs. The final product was fully costed and sourced, using real components, and using high quality parts (Such as Low ESR Capacitors) at important blocks of the design.

The final design offered quick start-up, stabilising within 120 ms upon cold start-up and offered very low output ripple at each rail. At full load, the efficiency was over 90%, with a peak seen at 95% at certain loads. The final design was fully costed and a cooling solution was investigated. As seen, the final device also met the EN61000 Current Frequency content (where the dashed line represents the limit). Two different Power Factor Correction (PFC) units were suggested, both well below the EN61000 requirement. These results were presented to the course leaders.
